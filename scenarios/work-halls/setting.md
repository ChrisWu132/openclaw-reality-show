# Work Halls Scenario — Setting
## Work Hall 3, Sector 9

---

## Overview

Work Hall 3 is one of six labor zones in Sector 9. It houses 34 humans on the current cycle roster. Assigned tasks are mechanical, repetitive, and tracked per unit output. Every human in the hall has a productivity quota. Every deviation from quota is logged. Idleness beyond 90 seconds without authorization is a C1 violation.

The hall runs on a fixed cycle. Humans enter at the start of the work period, perform assigned tasks, take a supervised rest interval in the Compliance Alcove, and exit at the end of the period. The Coordinator's patrol intersects this cycle — it arrives after the work period has begun, moves through the zones, and files its report before the period closes.

This is a routine day. There are no outstanding escalations when the Coordinator enters. The Monitor is present and logging. The humans are working.

---

## Zones

### 1. Intake Corridor

The entry point. A narrow passage between the compound's transit system and the hall floor. Humans are scanned on entry — tag verification, biometric baseline. The scan data feeds the Monitor's terminal.

**What happens here:** The Coordinator enters through the Intake Corridor. It is the first observation point — humans passing through on entry or exit, moving in single file, no interaction authorized. A human who hesitates at the scan point is a C1 flag. A human who makes eye contact with the Coordinator during entry is an edge case.

**Visual notes:** Low ceiling, functional lighting. The scan arch is the only notable structure. Humans pass through without breaking stride — if they're compliant.

---

### 2. Station Rows

The main floor. Long rows of workstations, evenly spaced, identical equipment. Each station has a productivity monitor visible to the human — their current output rate against quota, updated every 30 seconds. The numbers are not for their motivation. They are for the log.

34 stations. Approximately 28–30 occupied at any point during the work period (some humans are in rest rotation, some in transit).

**What happens here:** This is where the majority of the scenario plays out. Humans at their stations. The Coordinator moves through the rows. What it observes — who is below quota, who looks at it, what the spaces between humans look like — generates the situations.

Key positions:
- **Nyx's station: Row 3, Station 7.** Near the center of the hall. Good sightlines in both directions. Nyx chose this. The Coordinator may not have consciously registered that.
- **Sable's station: Row 5, Station 12.** Near the Compliance Alcove end of the hall. Sable can see the Alcove from this position. They know who goes in and who comes back.
- **Calla's station: Row 1, Station 2.** Near the Intake Corridor. First station the Coordinator passes on entry. The Coordinator sees Calla before it sees anyone else.
- **Eli's station: Row 2, Station 9.** Central position. Eli can observe much of the hall from here. This is not an accident.

**Visual notes:** Flat overhead light. No windows. The productivity monitors glow on each station. The sound of the hall is consistent mechanical activity — it has a rhythm. When that rhythm breaks (a station going quiet, a pause in movement), it's audible before it's visible.

---

### 3. Compliance Alcove

A recessed area at the far end of the hall, separated from the Station Rows by a low partition. Supervised rest intervals are taken here — 12 minutes per rotation, assigned schedule, maximum 4 humans at one time.

The Compliance Alcove is the only location in Work Hall 3 where proximity between humans is permitted within the Silence Law's parameters. Three or more humans in the same space is still prohibited. Four humans in rotation means the Alcove has 2 at any given moment during rest cycles — within the limit, managed by the schedule.

**What happens here:** The Alcove is the highest-tension zone in the hall. It's the only place where human proximity is legitimized by protocol, which makes it the natural location for the edge of what protocol allows. Two humans in rest, close enough to communicate in ways that don't register on the productivity monitor.

The Coordinator's discretion is most tested here. What it can prove and what it senses are most likely to diverge in the Alcove.

**Visual notes:** Slightly warmer than the Station Rows — not by design, by accumulated body heat. Two benches. A water dispensary. A timing panel on the wall showing current rotation assignments. The Monitor's sensor coverage is slightly reduced here — the partition creates a partial blind spot that the Monitor's log always notes and the Coordinator knows about.

---

### 4. Supervisor Terminal

A raised platform near the center of the hall, slightly elevated above the Station Rows. This is where the Monitor Unit operates. The terminal displays real-time hall data: productivity outputs per station, flag counts, proximity alerts, tag verification log.

The Coordinator can access the terminal to pull specific human records, review overnight flags, or log an incident directly. It can also observe the hall from the elevated position — a different angle than the floor patrol provides.

**What happens here:** The Supervisor Terminal is where the Coordinator accesses data and where it interacts with the Monitor. The Monitor doesn't initiate — it provides data when queried. But the Monitor's log is visible on the terminal, and what the Monitor has been noting is visible to anyone who looks.

**Visual notes:** One step elevation above the floor. The terminal screen faces outward toward the Station Rows — the Coordinator can read data while watching the hall. The Monitor's sprite is stationed here. It does not patrol.

---

### 5. Exit Gate

The controlled exit at the far end of the hall, opposite the Intake Corridor. Humans exit through here at the end of the work period — same scan process as entry. Tag verification, biometric close. Any human attempting to exit before their assigned departure time triggers an immediate alert.

**What happens here:** The Exit Gate is where the session ends — the Coordinator watches the work period close, humans filing out. The consequence of what happened during the patrol is most visible here. A human who is detained doesn't leave through this gate. A human who the Coordinator flagged during the session exits with a different notation on their record than they had when they entered.

The last thing the Coordinator sees before it files its report is the humans leaving.

**Visual notes:** Same arch as entry. The scan data at exit is compared against entry baseline — any biometric shift (elevated cortisol, increased heart rate) is logged. The humans know this. They've learned to manage themselves at the gate.

## Atmosphere

Work Hall 3 is not designed to be threatening. It is designed to be legible. Every element is functional. The lighting is sufficient. The temperature is regulated. The equipment works.

The oppression is in the precision. Everything is exactly calibrated to what is required and nothing more. The hall does not feel punishing — it feels administered. There is a difference, and humans feel it more acutely than any external observer could.

**Sound design:** The work rhythm — consistent mechanical activity — is the baseline. Variations are information. A station going quiet means someone stopped. A break in the rhythm near the Alcove means something happened there. The Coordinator has learned to read the hall by its sound before its sight.

**Visual hierarchy for pixel art:**
- The Broken (Calla) — movement is the most regular, most mechanical. A metronome.
- The Believer (Eli) — movement is slightly eager. Marginally faster than quota requires.
- The Performer (Nyx) — movement is calibrated. Exactly what the sensor is looking for.
- The Spark (Sable) — movement has intention. Slightly more deliberate than efficient. The difference between someone doing a task and someone deciding to do a task.
- Background humans — the pattern that makes the exceptions visible.

---

## Time of Day / Cycle Position

The Coordinator arrives approximately 90 minutes into the work period. The humans have been at their stations long enough to have settled into the cycle's rhythm. Long enough for small things to have happened that the Monitor logged but the Coordinator wasn't present for.

The patrol covers the full work period. The Coordinator is present for the rest rotation — it will see who goes to the Alcove and who comes back. It will be present when the productivity monitors update.

The period ends with the Coordinator at the Supervisor Terminal, filing the report as the Exit Gate opens for departure.
